Do Career Decisions Really Need to Follow Passion

    After entering university, all students will face a lifelong decision, which is what major to choose and what related industry to pursue in the future. This choice will undoubtedly affect a person's life. The most common saying we hear is that we must choose what we love as our profession and the career we will pursue for the rest of our lives. But I have a different perspective, and I think this statement is not comprehensive. This major choice definitely requires more consideration, and we need to consider this issue from many different angles. Our love and passion for a profession are often the least important conditions to consider. I think we should choose what is most suitable for ourselves as our profession and career. There are many drawbacks to choosing a career based on our own preferences and passions. Firstly, in "5 Reasons Following Your Passion May Not Be The Best Career Move," Brian T. King mentioned that following our passions can often be disappointing when making a choice. In reality, there are more complex factors that affect our career, so we should focus on considering other factors. Although passion can enhance motivation, it should not be the sole determinant of career decisions. At the same time, in Finding Your Passion Is Terrible Advice, Olga Khazan argues that human passion is not innate, but can be cultivated. Therefore, even if we are not currently interested in a profession, perhaps in the future we can cultivate it and become passionate about our work. last, Character Strengths: Person Environment Fit and Relationships with Job and Life Satisfaction is also mentioned, A high degree of consensus between individual character strengths and their work environment significantly enhances job satisfaction, reduces burnouts, and contributes to overall life satisfaction. Therefore, in my opinion, when searching for a career, we should choose what suits us, rather than what we like. because people's passion for something is not innate, it needs to be cultivated, when making career decisions, we must consider the benefits that a sense of achievement can bring. In sum, Career decisions should prioritize suitability and long-term growth, as passion can be cultivated and is not an innate determinant of success.

    Firstly, passion needs to be cultivated. People's passion for something is not innate, it needs to be cultivated. Even though we may not be interested in a profession right now, after a period of cultivation, we can still be passionate about it. If we simply believe that the career we like is the best and blindly choose the career we like, it will make our choices very limited, and it will be difficult for us to choose a career that truly suits us. "The problem with ‘find your passion’ is that it suggests that one’s calling is out there, Fully formed, and it's just waiting to be discovered. That's not how interests work. People develop passes by working hard at something. "(Khazan, 2018) Olga Khazan believes that passion is not something people are born with or need to discover. On the contrary, interest is cultivated through experience and effort. If we choose a career based on our interests, it will limit us to the preferences we already have and miss out on more and better career options. Therefore, waiting to 'find' one's passion can create unrealistic expectations and genuine career development and fulfillment. We should actively try more, cultivate new passions, and find a career that is more suitable for ourselves.

    On the other hand, it is also important to acknowledge the value of passion when choosing a career. When we choose a career, our own preferences are certainly within our own considerations, but we should not consider hobbies as the first criterion for senior choices. In "5 Reasons Following Your Passion May Not Be The Best Career Move," (Brian T. 2019) the author mentions "The most successful individuals often develop their passion through hard work and persistence, It can be seen that hobbies are only one of the secondary conditions we consider. We should develop our passion in the field that suits us, rather than letting existing passion limit our development. Meanwhile, Benjamin Todd, in his TEDx talk "To Find Work You Love, Don't Follow Your Passion," argues that passion can be developed by finding meaningful work that contributes to the well-being of others and aligns with our values. (Benjamin 2016) This method can bring a deeper and more sustainable passion beyond the initial interest. By contributing to larger causes, people often find themselves becoming more passionate and engaged in their work over time. Although passion should not be the only factor in career decisions, the value of passion cannot be denied. A possible counterargument would be that people could develop strengths in certain areas that they have passage for, desire struggling initially. However, without a series of advantages such as a sense of achievement as support, a failed career will be difficult for people to sustain in an industry for a long time.

    Finally, when making career decisions, we must consider the benefits that a sense of achievement can bring. In her TED Talk, "Grit: The Power of Passion and Perseverance," Angela Lee Duckworth emphasized that grit, defined as persistence and passion for long-term goals, is a major factor in success.( Angela 2013) In my opinion, the reason why we become passionate about something is that it combines our hobbies. On one hand, it can bring us a sense of achievement and greatly enhance our passion for doing something. For example, two people who had never studied mathematics before, when they first started learning mathematics, one of them could easily solve problems that were difficult for others, while the other person couldn't. It is obvious that the first person is more likely to develop a passion for learning mathematics. And this first person is obviously very suitable for learning mathematics. And this is the benefit that choosing a profession and career that suits oneself can bring. Choosing a career solely based on current interests may lead us to give up too easily when facing challenges, while choosing a career that matches our strengths and cultivates perseverance will enable us to develop in the long run. In real life, there are a large number of people who choose computer and business majors, because in the eyes of most people, these two majors will have more ideal employment prospects in the future. Don't all those who choose these majors have other majors that they love more? Are people working in these fields particularly fond of this profession? So it is obvious that most people do not follow their own preferences when facing decisions that determine their future. As we become more proficient and confident in our abilities, passion will grow over time, making courage and adaptability more important in career choices than initial interests.
